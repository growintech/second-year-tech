Problem to Solution

Choose a track, analyse the problem in depth, define your target audience, and write a clear problem statement with your startup name.

📅 Deadline — 3 April (both schools)

This is the foundation of your entire project. Take time to really understand the problem before jumping to a solution. A strong analysis here makes everything easier later.

The 5 Tracks

Choose one track as your starting point. The tracks don't tell you what to build — they point to a problem to explore. Open each card to read the context and questions to guide your thinking.

Context

Phones and social media are designed to keep us glued to the screen. Everyone knows they should be studying, sleeping or exercising, but often ends up doomscrolling for hours, losing control of their time.

Stimulus questions
  • What is the exact moment or emotion that makes you pick up your phone for no reason?
  • Why do focus apps (like Pomodoro timers) often get abandoned after a couple of days?
  • How could you make deep studying something students want to do, not something they have to do?

Step 1Choose a Track & Analyse the Problem

Read the 5 tracks above and pick the one that interests you most. Two pairs on the same track can arrive at completely different solutions.

Build a Problem Tree

A problem tree is a visual diagram that breaks a problem into its root causes (below) and effects (above). It helps you move from a vague feeling ("transport is annoying") to specific, solvable sub-problems.

  • 1
    Write the core problem in the centre
    One sentence that captures the main problem from the track you chose.
  • 2
    Dig into root causes (below the problem)
    Ask "Why does this happen?" repeatedly. Go at least 2–3 levels deep. Each cause should be specific and verifiable.
  • 3
    Map the effects (above the problem)
    Ask "What happens because of this?" These effects are what your pitch deck will use to show why the problem matters.
🎥
Video tutorial. Watch the Problem Tree tutorial ↗ for a step-by-step walkthrough. Use XMind or Miro to build it digitally, or draw it on paper.

Define your target audience

Don't say "young people" or "everyone." Your target should be a specific person with a specific problem. Think about:

  • Age range, location, daily routine
  • What frustrates them about the current situation
  • What solutions they've already tried (and why they failed)
  • How willing they are to try something new

Analyse 2–3 possible solutions

Before choosing your final solution, brainstorm at least 2–3 different approaches. For each one, think about:

  • Is it technically feasible for you to build a landing page for it?
  • Does it solve the root cause, or just a symptom?
  • Would real people actually use it?

Step 2Problem Statement & Startup Name

Write the Problem Statement

The problem statement is max 10 lines and must answer two fundamental questions:

  • 1
    What does the startup do?
    Describe the solution clearly. What does it offer? How does it work at a high level? What makes it different from what already exists?
  • 2
    Who uses it?
    Describe your target user. Be specific — this should match the target analysis you did in Step 1.
📖
Need inspiration? Browse the 5 startup examples ↗ to see how other teams structured their problem statements.

Name your startup

Give your startup a name — it can be provisional. A good name is short, memorable, and hints at what the product does. Don't overthink it — you can always change it later.

Step 3Share on WhatsApp

Send your problem statement to the class group

Once you've chosen your track and written your problem statement, only one member of the group must send the following message in the class WhatsApp group:

Hi! I'm [First Name Last Name]. I have chosen Track [N] — [Title]. My problem statement is: [ONE SENTENCE describing the specific problem you want to address].
⚠️
Only one message per group. One member of the group sends the message on behalf of the whole team. Make sure everyone has agreed on the chosen track and problem before sending.
💡
Example: "Hi! I'm Marco Rossi. I have chosen Track 4 — The Commuter Nightmare. My problem statement is: Students living outside Turin waste over 90 minutes a day commuting because there is no easy way to coordinate shared rides with classmates on the same route."
