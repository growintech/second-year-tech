The 5 Tracks

Choose one track as your starting point. The tracks don't tell you what to build — they point to a problem to explore. Open each card to read the context and questions to guide your thinking.

Context

Phones and social media are designed to keep us glued to the screen. Everyone knows they should be studying, sleeping or exercising, but often ends up doomscrolling for hours, losing control of their time.

The real problem

Current blocking and "productivity" apps feel like punishments — they lock your screen without solving the need to switch off. There's no way to regain focus that feels rewarding rather than frustrating.

Stimulus questions
  • What is the exact moment or emotion that makes you pick up your phone for no reason?
  • Why do focus apps (like Pomodoro timers) often get abandoned after a couple of days?
  • How could you make deep studying something students want to do, not something they have to do?
Context

Pocket money, summer jobs, birthday gifts: teenagers handle small amounts of money, but nobody teaches them how to manage it. Money often disappears quickly through micro-transactions, forgotten subscriptions and impulse purchases.

The real problem

Saving money is seen as an "adult" concept. Traditional banking apps don't speak the language of an 18-year-old who wants to set aside money for a trip or a new laptop without feeling judged.

Stimulus questions
  • Why is it so hard to consistently put aside even just €20–50 a month?
  • What are the "invisible" expenses that drain students' money without them noticing?
  • How could you turn saving into an automatic habit rather than a painful one?
Context

Many students would like to do small jobs for financial independence: tutoring, dog-sitting, video editing, tech help for elderly people, graphic design for local shops.

The real problem

Finding these opportunities still relies entirely on chance, posters in bars or word of mouth from parents. There's no local, safe and reliable "marketplace" where young people can offer their micro-skills without being exploited.

Stimulus questions
  • What can you (or a friend) do that someone in your neighbourhood would be willing to pay for?
  • What stops a parent or adult from trusting a student with a small job?
  • How would you create a system that guarantees trust, safety and clear payments between both parties?
Context

Late buses, missed connections, strikes or parents forced to play taxi at impossible hours. For many students, getting to school, the gym or a party is a daily logistical challenge.

The real problem

Shared mobility solutions (car sharing, rental scooters) are expensive and often off-limits to minors. Carpooling between classmates would be the obvious solution, but organising it is chaotic and leads to awkwardness.

Stimulus questions
  • How much time do you lose every day commuting, and how much frustration does it cause?
  • Why is it so difficult to ask for or offer a lift to someone doing the same journey every morning?
  • How could you make this kind of sharing fair, organised and free from awkwardness?
Context

You want to try playing bass, learn film photography, try climbing or experiment with 3D printing. But signing up for a class and buying the equipment costs hundreds of euros.

The real problem

Exploring new hobbies has a very high entry barrier — economic and organisational. This kills curiosity: if a student isn't 100% sure they'll like it, they won't even try. Meanwhile, the world is full of unused equipment gathering dust.

Stimulus questions
  • What is a hobby you gave up on trying because it was too expensive or complicated to start?
  • How could you lower this initial economic barrier?
  • How would you connect people who have equipment and experience with people who just want to "try it for a weekend"?

Goals, Teamwork & Creativity 40 pts
Depth of problem analysis, originality of the solution, quality of the problem statement and pitch deck, and how effectively the pair worked together toward a convincing idea.
Guidelines 30 pts
Following the project requirements: delivering each phase on time, meeting the technical specifications (countdown, interactive feature, responsive layout), maintaining the development diary, and collecting ≥ 20 validation responses.
Presentation 20 pts
Clarity and confidence of the oral presentation, effective use of the pitch deck (not reading from slides), live demo of the working website, and ability to handle questions from the instructors.
Code Quality 10 pts
Clean, readable HTML/CSS/JS. Students must be able to explain every line — including any code generated with AI. Points are lost if the code was copied without understanding.

How-to guides

  • Ask about the problem, not the solution — at first.
    ❌ "Would you use an app that helps you manage your money?"
    ✅ "How often do you reach the end of the month without knowing how you spent your money?"
  • Use numerical scales for intensity questions — they give you quantifiable data you can cite in the pitch deck.
  • Include at least one open question — open answers give you insights you'd never have thought to ask for.
  • Briefly explain your idea before asking if people would use it — 2–3 lines about the startup, then ask.
  • Keep it under 8–10 questions — beyond that, people abandon the form or answer randomly.

The development diary documents your process as you build the website. It is not a summary written at the end — update it step by step as you work.

  • What to include: what you tried to do, what tools or techniques you used, what problems you ran into, and how you solved them.
  • If you used AI: note what prompt you gave, what the AI produced, and what you changed or didn't understand at first.
  • Format: a Google Doc with dated entries is fine. Screenshots of your work in progress are a plus.
  • Three deliveries: first version (Phase 2), second version (Phase 3), final version (Final Submission).
