Phase Overview

📅 Deadline — 3 April

Phase 1 — Problem to Solution. Choose a track. Build a Problem Tree to break the problem down and identify root causes. Define your target audience. Write a clear problem statement (max 10 lines) and give your startup a name.

📅 8 / 10 April

Phase 2 — Form & Landing Page. Validate your idea with a form (Google Forms / Tally) — collect ≥ 20 responses and comment on the results. Build the first version of your startup website (HTML, CSS, JS, responsive, with navbar). Start the development diary.

📝
Individual test on the same day. The individual JavaScript test covers concepts studied during the course — separate from the project work.
📅 15 / 17 April

Phase 3 — Website Progress. Advance the website with two mandatory JS features: a live countdown using Date() + setInterval(), and at least one additional interactive element (FAQ accordion, slider, contact form with alert, dark mode toggle, etc.).

🤖
AI is allowed. You can use ChatGPT, Claude, etc. But you must be able to explain every single line during the presentation.
📅 Final: 20/22 April 🎤 Presentations: 22/24 April

Phase 4 — Final Submission & Presentation. Upload all final materials (slides, website ZIP, pitch deck PDF, final diary) to Google Classroom. Then present your startup to the class in 5–7 minutes: pitch deck walkthrough, live website demo, and Q&A with instructors.

Grading Rubric — 100 points

Goals, Teamwork & Creativity 40 pts
Depth of problem analysis, originality of the solution, quality of the problem statement and pitch deck, and how effectively the pair worked together toward a convincing idea.
Guidelines 30 pts
Following the project requirements: delivering each phase on time, meeting the technical specifications (countdown, interactive feature, responsive layout), maintaining the development diary, and collecting ≥ 20 validation responses.
Presentation 20 pts
Clarity and confidence of the oral presentation, effective use of the pitch deck (not reading from slides), live demo of the working website, and ability to handle questions from the instructors.
Code Quality 10 pts
Clean, readable HTML/CSS/JS. Students must be able to explain every line — including any code generated with AI. Points are lost if the code was copied without understanding.

How-to guides

  • Ask about the problem, not the solution — at first.
    ❌ "Would you use an app that helps you manage your money?"
    ✅ "How often do you reach the end of the month without knowing how you spent your money?"
  • Use numerical scales for intensity questions — they give you quantifiable data you can cite in the pitch deck.
  • Include at least one open question — open answers give you insights you'd never have thought to ask for.
  • Briefly explain your idea before asking if people would use it — 2–3 lines about the startup, then ask.
  • Keep it under 8–10 questions — beyond that, people abandon the form or answer randomly.

The development diary documents your process as you build the website. It is not a summary written at the end — update it step by step as you work.

  • What to include: what you tried to do, what tools or techniques you used, what problems you ran into, and how you solved them.
  • If you used AI: note what prompt you gave, what the AI produced, and what you changed or didn't understand at first.
  • Format: a Google Doc with dated entries is fine. Screenshots of your work in progress are a plus.
  • Three deliveries: first version (Phase 2), second version (Phase 3), final version (Final Submission).

Use PerplexityAI Deep Research with a precise prompt to get numerical market data with verifiable sources. Every number in your pitch deck must have a source.
