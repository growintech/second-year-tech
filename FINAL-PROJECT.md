# Final Project 2026 — Grow in Tech (Year 2)

The Final Project is the closing project of the second year. Unlike the Mid Year Project — which was done in groups of more — **this project is done in pairs**. Each pair builds their own fictional startup, from problem definition to a working website.

By the end you will have delivered:
- A **Problem Tree** with problem analysis and target definition
- A written **Elevator Pitch** with your startup name
- A **validation form** with at least 20 responses and a short comment
- A **pitch deck** (3–4 slides) with market analysis and competitor research
- A **working website** in HTML, CSS and JavaScript
- An **oral presentation** in front of the class

---

## The Tracks

Choose one of the 5 tracks below as your starting point. The tracks describe a problem to analyse — your startup will come from your own analysis of that problem. Two pairs who choose the same track can arrive at completely different solutions.

---

### Track 1 — The Attention War

**Context**
Phones and social media are designed to keep us glued to the screen. Everyone knows they should be studying, sleeping or exercising, but often ends up doomscrolling for hours, losing control of their time.

**The real problem**
Current blocking and "productivity" apps feel like punishments — they lock your screen without solving the need to switch off. There's no way to regain focus that feels rewarding rather than frustrating.

**Stimulus questions**
- What is the exact moment or emotion that makes you pick up your phone for no reason?
- Why do focus apps (like Pomodoro timers) often get abandoned after a couple of days?
- How could you make deep studying something students *want* to do, not something they *have* to do?

---

### Track 2 — The Missing Financial Education

**Context**
Pocket money, summer jobs, birthday gifts: teenagers handle small amounts of money, but nobody teaches them how to manage it. Money often disappears quickly through micro-transactions, forgotten subscriptions and impulse purchases.

**The real problem**
Saving money is seen as an "adult" concept. Traditional banking apps don't speak the language of an 18-year-old who wants to set aside money for a trip or a new laptop without feeling judged.

**Stimulus questions**
- Why is it so hard to consistently put aside even just €20–50 a month?
- What are the "invisible" expenses that drain students' money without them noticing?
- How could you turn saving into an automatic habit rather than a painful one?

---

### Track 3 — The Invisible Market (and Talent)

**Context**
Many students would like to do small jobs for financial independence: tutoring, dog-sitting, video editing, tech help for elderly people, graphic design for local shops.

**The real problem**
Finding these opportunities still relies entirely on chance, posters in bars or word of mouth from parents. There's no local, safe and reliable "marketplace" where young people can offer their micro-skills without being exploited.

**Stimulus questions**
- What can you (or a friend) do that someone in your neighbourhood would be willing to pay for?
- What stops a parent or adult from trusting a student with a small job?
- How would you create a system that guarantees trust, safety and clear payments between both parties?

---

### Track 4 — The Commuter Nightmare

**Context**
Late buses, missed connections, strikes or parents forced to play taxi at impossible hours. For many students, getting to school, the gym or a party is a daily logistical challenge.

**The real problem**
Shared mobility solutions (car sharing, rental scooters) are expensive and often off-limits to minors. Carpooling between classmates would be the obvious solution, but organising it is chaotic and leads to awkwardness ("who pays for petrol?").

**Stimulus questions**
- How much time do you lose every day commuting, and how much frustration does it cause?
- Why is it so difficult to ask for or offer a lift to someone doing the same journey every morning?
- How could you make this kind of sharing fair, organised and free from the awkwardness of asking for money?

---

### Track 5 — The Passion Paywall

**Context**
You want to try playing bass, learn film photography, try climbing or experiment with 3D printing. But signing up for a class and buying the equipment costs hundreds of euros.

**The real problem**
Exploring new hobbies has a very high entry barrier — economic and organisational. This kills curiosity: if a student isn't 100% sure they'll like it, they won't even try. Meanwhile, the world is full of unused equipment gathering dust and people willing to share the basics of their passion.

**Stimulus questions**
- What is a hobby you gave up on trying because it was too expensive or complicated to start?
- How could you lower this initial economic barrier?
- How would you connect people who have the equipment and experience with people who just want to "try it for a weekend"?

## Phase 1 — From Problem to Solution

**Deadlines:** Sommelier: 30 March | Monti: 1 April

### 1️⃣ Step 1 – Choose a track and build the Problem Tree

Choose one of the 5 tracks. Build your Problem Tree (using XMind, Miro, or pen and paper) to break the problem down and identify 2–3 root causes. Define your target audience as specifically as possible — not "young people" but a real, specific person with a real, specific problem.

💡 Watch the [Problem Tree tutorial](https://www.loom.com/share/70c6057d71804a31b80c1e556cf60462) before you start. You can also browse the [5 complete startup examples](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1hgVrsJcedr0YYf8Uj0D5F3D66FXvJRKHmBa6huLaQ4U/edit?usp=sharing) to get a feel for how strong problem trees look in practice.

### 2️⃣ Step 2 – Choose a solution and write the Elevator Pitch

From the root causes in your tree, identify 2–3 possible solutions and choose the strongest one. Write your Elevator Pitch (max 10 lines) answering three questions: *What does the product do? Who uses it? What makes it different from what already exists?* Give your startup a name — provisional is fine.

### To be delivered by the deadline on Google Classroom

1. **Problem Tree** (PDF or photo) with a written description of your target audience
2. **Written Elevator Pitch** and startup name

## Phase 2 — Validation and Pitch Deck

**Deadlines:** Sommelier: 3 April | Monti: 6 April

### 1️⃣ Step 1 – Market validation form

Create a form (Google Forms or Tally) to find out whether other people feel the problem and would use your solution. Collect **at least 20 responses**. Write a short comment (5–10 lines) on what you learned from the results.

Before building the form, read the **How to build an effective validation form** section below. You can also look at the [startup examples document](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1hgVrsJcedr0YYf8Uj0D5F3D66FXvJRKHmBa6huLaQ4U/edit?usp=sharing) — it includes complete form examples for 5 different startups.

### 2️⃣ Step 2 – Pitch Deck (3–4 slides)

Create a presentation on Google Slides or Canva. It must include:
1. **Cover & Problem/Target** — your problem statement and who it affects
2. **Solution** — what your product does
3. **Market & Competitors** — use PerplexityAI Deep Research to find TAM/SAM/SOM data and 1–2 competitors. Every number must have a source. See the [Deep Research prompt template](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1SaYaIgQMiizNfCSuKkDxed3XPUquUWxQQWkTpRgeXZc/edit?usp=sharing) and the [TAM/SAM/SOM graph example](https://advertising.amazon.com/it-it/library/guides/tam-sam-som#1a).
4. **Call to Action / Conclusion** — why your idea wins

Browse the [startup examples](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1hgVrsJcedr0YYf8Uj0D5F3D66FXvJRKHmBa6huLaQ4U/edit?usp=drive_link) for reference on what a strong pitch deck looks like.

### To be delivered by the deadline on Google Classroom

1. Link to the form, screenshot of the results, and short comment
2. Definitive pitch deck (maximum 3–4 slides, exported as PDF)

## Phase 3 — The Website

**Deadlines:** Sommelier: 8 April | Monti: 10 April

### 1️⃣ Step 1 – Build the website

Build a website in HTML, CSS and JavaScript. It must have a responsive layout, a working navbar, and clear sections that communicate your startup's problem, solution and product.

**Mandatory JavaScript requirements:**
1. **Launch countdown** using `Date()` and `setInterval()`
2. **At least one additional interactive feature** (e.g. contact form with alert, FAQ accordion, slider, embedded map, etc.)

You can look at the [website examples document](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1hgVrsJcedr0YYf8Uj0D5F3D66FXvJRKHmBa6huLaQ4U/edit?usp=drive_link) for inspiration.

**Use of AI:** You are allowed to use AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) to help generate and structure your website code. However, you must be able to **explain every single line of code** during the oral presentation.

### 2️⃣ Step 2 – Prepare the oral presentation

Your oral presentation must follow this order:
1. **Pitch Deck** — problem, solution, market data
2. **Website demo** — walk through the site live and demonstrate the interactive JS feature
3. **Q&A** — instructors may ask you to explain specific parts of your code

Before your presentation, read the [delivery techniques guide](https://docs.google.com/document/u/0/d/15yfYb_NzsoJEd4fyeid9TJfXwSb2sMdCq_b93tZdjTU/edit) and practise out loud. You will have limited time to rehearse in class.

### To be delivered by the deadline on Google Classroom

1. `.zip` folder containing all website files (or a link to GitHub Pages / JSBin)

## How to build an effective validation form

- **Ask questions about the problem, not the solution** (at least at first)
  - ❌ Wrong: "Would you use an app that helps you manage your money?"
  - ✅ Right: "How often do you reach the end of the month without knowing how you spent your money?"
- **Use numerical scales for intensity questions** — they give you quantifiable data
- **Include at least one open question** — open answers give you insights you'd never have thought to ask for
- **Briefly explain your idea before asking if people would use it** — 2–3 lines about your startup, then ask
- **Keep it under 8–10 questions** — beyond that, people abandon the form or answer randomly

## How to use PerplexityAI for market research

Use **PerplexityAI Deep Research** with a precise prompt to get numerical data with verifiable sources. Every number you put in the pitch deck must have a source — PerplexityAI provides them automatically.

You can find the full ready-to-use template in the [Deep Research prompt document](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1SaYaIgQMiizNfCSuKkDxed3XPUquUWxQQWkTpRgeXZc/edit?usp=sharing). The output will give you TAM, SAM and SOM figures with citations — take the key numbers and their sources and insert them into your pitch deck slides.
