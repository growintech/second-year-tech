The 5 tracks

Choose one track as your starting point. Open each card to read the context, the real problem, and questions to guide your thinking.

Context

Phones and social media are designed to keep us glued to the screen. Everyone knows they should be studying, sleeping or exercising, but often ends up doomscrolling for hours.

The real problem

Current "productivity" apps feel like punishments — they lock your screen without solving the need to switch off. There's no way to regain focus that feels rewarding rather than frustrating.

Stimulus questions
  • What is the exact moment that makes you pick up your phone for no reason?
  • Why do focus apps often get abandoned after a couple of days?
  • How could you make deep studying something students want to do?
Context

Teenagers handle money — pocket money, summer jobs, gifts — but nobody teaches them how to manage it. It disappears through micro-transactions and forgotten subscriptions.

The real problem

Saving is seen as an "adult" concept. Banking apps don't speak the language of an 18-year-old who wants to set aside money for a trip without feeling judged.

Stimulus questions
  • Why is it so hard to consistently put aside even just €20–50 a month?
  • What are the "invisible" expenses that drain students' money without them noticing?
  • How could you turn saving into an automatic habit rather than a painful one?
Context

Many students would like to do small jobs for financial independence: tutoring, dog-sitting, video editing, tech help for elderly people, graphic design for local shops.

The real problem

Finding these opportunities relies entirely on chance or word of mouth. There's no local, safe "marketplace" where young people can offer their skills without being exploited.

Stimulus questions
  • What can you do that someone in your neighbourhood would be willing to pay for?
  • What stops a parent from trusting a student with a small job?
  • How would you guarantee trust, safety and clear payments between both parties?
Context

Late buses, missed connections, strikes. For many students, getting to school or a party is a daily logistical challenge.

The real problem

Shared mobility is expensive and off-limits to minors. Carpooling between classmates would work, but organising it is chaotic ("who pays for petrol?").

Stimulus questions
  • How much time do you lose commuting every day?
  • Why is it difficult to ask for or offer a lift to someone on the same journey?
  • How could you make sharing fair and free from awkwardness?
Context

You want to try playing bass, learn film photography, or experiment with 3D printing. But signing up and buying equipment costs hundreds of euros.

The real problem

Hobbies have a high entry barrier. If a student isn't 100% sure they'll like it, they won't try. Meanwhile, unused equipment gathers dust and people would love to share their skills.

Stimulus questions
  • What hobby did you give up on because it was too expensive to start?
  • How could you lower the initial economic barrier?
  • How would you connect people with equipment with people who just want to try it?

Grading rubric — 100 points

Phase 1 — Problem analysis and solution15 pts
Problem & Target
Insufficient · 0–5
Shallow tree, generic target (e.g. "young people").
Sufficient · 6–10
Tree identifies causes; target defined but not very specific.
Excellent · 11–15
Logical, deep tree. Extremely specific, real target.
Solution (Pitch)
Insufficient · 0–5
Solution unclear or disconnected from the problem.
Sufficient · 6–10
Solution understandable but weak differentiation.
Excellent · 11–15
Clear, differentiated solution convincingly explained.
Phase 2 — Validation and Pitch Deck35 pts
Validation data
Insufficient · 0–11
Fewer than 10 responses or poorly structured form. No comment.
Sufficient · 12–24
10–19 responses. Basic comment on the data.
Excellent · 25–35
20+ responses. Critical comment on what was learned.
Pitch Deck
Insufficient · 0–11
Key slides missing or no sources for data.
Sufficient · 12–24
Slides present but visually weak. Partial market data.
Excellent · 25–35
3–4 polished slides, data with sources, clear competitor analysis.
Phase 3 — Website35 pts
Structure (HTML/CSS)
Insufficient · 0–11

Sufficient · 12–24
Working site with mobile issues. Basic design.
Excellent · 25–35
Clean, fully responsive (Grid/Flexbox), consistent design.
JavaScript
Insufficient · 0–11
